# AIMSEA Problem Statement

**Document Identifier:** AIMSEA-PS-001

**Version:** 0.1

**Status:** Draft

This document defines the educational problem that motivates the AIMSEA research project.

---

# 1. The Assessment Problem

Educators are responsible for developing assessment systems that produce evidence from which they can make informed judgments regarding learner proficiency. Those judgments should be intellectually defensible.

In rapidly evolving technical disciplines, particularly AI-enabled mobile software engineering, maintaining effective assessment systems has become increasingly difficult. Course content, development tools, programming frameworks, professional practices, and learner capabilities evolve continuously, requiring assessment systems to be revised more frequently than traditional development processes were designed to support.

This document refers to this challenge as **the Assessment Problem**.

---

# 2. Why the Problem Matters

Assessment systems influence educational decisions affecting learners, educators, academic institutions, employers, and society. Grades, certifications, and other measures of learner proficiency should therefore be supported by assessment systems that produce defensible evidence.

As technical disciplines evolve more rapidly, educators face increasing pressure to update assessment systems while maintaining their quality, consistency, and intellectual defensibility. Time and resource limitations often make this difficult for individual educators working independently.

---

# 3. Why the Problem Is Difficult

Several factors contribute to the Assessment Problem, including:

- Rapid evolution of technical knowledge and professional practice.
- Frequent changes to software development tools, frameworks, and programming languages.
- Artificial intelligence systems that change both what learners should know and how they demonstrate proficiency.
- Limited faculty time and institutional resources available for assessment development and maintenance.
- The need to frequently revise assessment systems while preserving consistency, quality, and defensibility.

These challenges are particularly evident in AI-enabled mobile software engineering education, which serves as AIMSEA's initial application domain.

---

# 4. Existing Approaches and Their Limitations

Educators currently employ a wide variety of assessment methods, including examinations, programming assignments, project-based learning, rubrics, competency frameworks, accreditation standards, and peer review. These approaches provide valuable mechanisms for assessing learner proficiency.

However, relatively little support exists for the timely, collaborative development, evaluation, revision, organization, and scholarly review of assessment systems and assessment artifacts. As a result, many educators repeatedly develop similar assessment materials independently, often with limited opportunities for peer review, empirical validation, or long-term maintenance.

---

# 5. AIMSEA's Contribution

AIMSEA proposes a collaborative methodology, repository, and scholarly community through which educators can develop, evaluate, revise, organize, share, and peer-review assessment systems and assessment artifacts.

The project's objective is to support the development of assessment systems that produce defensible evidence of learner proficiency while remaining practical for educators working within real-world constraints of time and available resources.

---

# 6. Scope and Limitations

AIMSEA is initially focused on AI-enabled mobile software engineering education. This application domain was selected because it exemplifies the challenges associated with rapidly evolving technical disciplines.

The methodology is not currently claimed to apply universally across all educational disciplines. Whether AIMSEA's principles can be successfully generalized to other domains remains an open research question.

Likewise, AIMSEA does not replace educator judgment. Rather, it seeks to assist educators in developing assessment systems that produce evidence from which informed educational judgments may be made.
